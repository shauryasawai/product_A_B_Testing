Product Experiment · Payment Screen
Can simplifying what users see before paying boost conversions?
Right now, Grip shows users a full investment summary, issuer details, yield, risk rating, tax implications and right before they hit "Pay". The theory: too much information at the wrong moment creates hesitation. This A/B test checks whether showing less (but keeping details accessible) makes more users complete their investment.

Part A · Test Design
How many users do we need? How long should we run?
The sample size formula ensures our result isn't just luck — it's real
📐 The Maths — Sample Size Formula
💡
In plain English: We need enough users in each group so that if a real difference exists, we'll actually detect it — and not mistake random luck for a real finding.
Formulan = (Zα/2 + Zβ)² × [p₁(1−p₁) + p₂(1−p₂)] / (p₁−p₂)²
p₁ = Control conversion0.48 (current 48%)
p₂ = Variant target0.53 (48% + 5pp MDE)
Zα/2 at 95% confidence1.96
Zβ at 80% power0.84  →  Sum = 2.80, Sum² = 7.84
Numerator7.84 × [0.2496 + 0.2491] = 7.84 × 0.4987 = 3.910
Denominator(0.05)² = 0.0025
Resultn = 1,564 users per variant
📅
How long to run? 15,000 users/month ÷ 30 days = ~500/day reaching payment screen. Split 50/50 = 250 per variant per day. 1,564 ÷ 250 = ~6.3 days minimum. We recommend 14 days to capture full weekday/weekend behaviour differences.

🎲 Randomisation — User-level or Session-level?
✅
Answer: User-level randomisation. Assign each user to Control or Variant once, and keep them there for the entire test duration.
1
Why not session-level?
If we assigned randomly per visit, the same user could see the full summary on Monday and the simplified screen on Tuesday. That's a contaminated experiment — their behaviour is influenced by both versions, making it impossible to know which one caused a conversion.
2
Investment decisions take multiple visits
A typical Grip user might browse products on day 1, compare options on day 3, and finally pay on day 7. User-level assignment ensures they always see the same experience throughout this journey — making the test reflect real-world conditions.
3
How to implement
Hash the user_id to deterministically assign each user to a variant on their first payment screen visit. Store the assignment in their profile so every subsequent session shows the same version.
🛡️ Guardrail Metrics — "First, Do No Harm"
💡
What are guardrail metrics? Safety checks that ensure improving conversion doesn't accidentally cause harm elsewhere — like pushing users to invest without understanding what they're buying.
1
📋 "View Details" expansion rate
What % of Variant B users tapped to expand full details before paying? If this is very low, users are investing without reading — a SEBI compliance risk. Threshold: alert if below 20%.
2
🎟️ Support ticket rate (post-investment)
How many users contact support with confusion about their investment within 7 days? Queries like "what is this product?" or "when can I withdraw?" signal uninformed decisions. No more than 10% relative increase vs Control.
3
🔄 30-day early withdrawal rate
Are Variant B investors more likely to try exiting positions early? Regret-driven exits suggest users didn't fully understand lock-in or terms. This is a delayed but powerful signal of real harm.

🎯 Did we hit the MDE? Understanding the gap
⚠️
MDE is not a cliff edge. The Minimum Detectable Effect (5pp) was our pre-specified "minimum we care about". Observing 4pp doesn't mean the variant is useless — it means the true effect is likely somewhere in the 2–6pp range. It's still a real, significant, business-meaningful improvement.
🚦 Decision Framework — Ship, Iterate, or Kill?
Walking through the decision systematically
✅ Statistically significant
p=0.04 < 0.05. We can confidently say the improvement is real and not random noise. This is a green light.
✅ Economically meaningful
+600 extra completions per month. Even at modest average investment ticket sizes, this represents real, recurring revenue for Grip.
⚠️ Below 5pp MDE target
4pp < 5pp. But the MDE was our input assumption — a floor, not a law. Being 1pp below doesn't negate the value of the improvement.
⚠️ Guardrail check required
Before shipping, we MUST verify the three guardrail metrics are healthy. If expansion rate or ticket rate looks bad, we iterate first.
⚖️ Final Verdict
Conditional ship + iterate. Ship Variant B as the new default IF guardrail metrics show no harm. Do not kill the variant — that would throw away a statistically significant, economically material win. Simultaneously, design Variant C to push conversion above 5pp.
⚖️ The Regulatory Risk — Are users investing without reading the fine print?
🚨
The colleague's concern: "Variant B users are investing without reading risk details — we may be creating uninformed investments, which is a regulatory risk (SEBI mandates risk disclosure)."
📊 What the guardrail data would show
Hypothetical: if "View Details" expansion in Variant B dropped dramatically, it would confirm the risk
🔍 What to check before shipping
"View Details" expansion rate in Variant B — If <20% of Variant B completers ever expanded details, the hypothesis is confirmed and we must not ship as-is.
Time-on-screen for B completers — If median seconds on payment screen dropped sharply (e.g. 45s → 12s), users are rushing through without reading.
Support tickets tagged "terms confusion" — A spike in "what is this?" or "when can I withdraw?" queries 7 days post-investment is direct evidence of uninformed decisions.
⚠️
If guardrails fail: Redesign Variant B to surface a mandatory 3-line risk summary inline (not hidden behind a tap) — preserving informed consent while still reducing information overload. This becomes Variant C.

Part B · Follow-up
Variant C — The next experiment to push above 5pp
Threading the needle between simplicity and informed consent
🅒 Variant C · Progressive Disclosure with Inline Risk Nudge
💡 Hypothesis
Users aren't opposed to risk details — they're opposed to information overload at the moment of payment. A scannable 3-line risk card shown always (not hidden) reduces friction AND maintains informed consent.
🖥️ UI Change
Show: investment amount + expected return (like B). But instead of a "View Details" collapse, show an always-visible 3-line card: (a) Risk rating badge, (b) Lock-in period, (c) Tax in one line. Below: "Full prospectus →" (link, not CTA-blocking).
📏 Success Metrics
Primary: Payment completion rate (target ≥52%, ideally ≥53%)

Guardrails: "Full prospectus" click rate, support ticket rate, 30-day withdrawal rate — all must be ≥ Variant B levels.
🎯
Why this is better than B: Variant B hides information (regulatory risk). Variant C curates information — showing only the 3 things users need to know at point of payment, always visible, without the full overload. Best of both worlds: simpler AND compliant.

Part C · Broader Product Thinking
Where else in the funnel can we apply "reduce cognitive load"?
If simplifying the payment screen works, the same principle applies upstream — show users what they need for the decision in front of them, not everything at once
🔑 The Core Insight from this A/B Test
💡
Users who reach the payment screen have already decided to invest. They're past the education phase. Showing them a full investment summary again is optimising for the wrong goal — re-informing someone who's already committed. The same misalignment exists at other funnel stages: too much info for the stage the user is actually in.
🏗️ The Full Funnel — Where Friction Lives
🎯 2 Other Funnel Stages to Simplify
Stage 1 — Browse / Discovery
Simplify product listing cards
Problem: Each card shows 6–7 data points — yield, tenure, issuer, credit rating, risk label, liquidity, minimum amount — before the user has even expressed intent.

Hypothesis: Reduce browse cards to just yield + tenure (+ risk badge). Defer everything else to the product detail page.

Metric: Browse → product detail CTR.
Guardrail: Product detail → payment entry rate.
Stage 2 — KYC / Onboarding
Chunk the KYC form into a wizard
Problem: New users face a long KYC scroll — PAN, Aadhaar, bank details, nominee, risk profiling — all at once. This is the biggest upstream drop-off point.

Hypothesis: A 4-step wizard (identity → bank → risk profile → review) with a progress indicator will reduce abandonment by making each step feel lightweight and the end feel near.

Metric: KYC start → completion rate.
Guardrail: Time to first investment post-KYC.
🗝️
The golden rule: At each stage, only show the information needed for the decision the user is making right now. Browsing = "Is this interesting?" (yield + tenure). Committing = "How much will I earn on X rupees?" (amount + return). Everything else is friction.
